ABSTRACT
High-throughput DNA sequencing has revealed millions of single nucleotide variants (SNVs) in the human genome, with a small fraction linked to disease. The effect of missense variants, which alter the protein sequence, is particularly challenging to interpret due to the scarcity of clinical annotations and experimental information. Current prediction tools use conservation and structural information and have limited performance in predicting variant pathogenicity. In this study, we explored the pathogenicity of homologous missense variants—variants in equivalent positions across homologous proteins— focusing on proteins involved in autosomal dominant diseases. By analyzing 2,976 pathogenic and 17,555 non-pathogenic homologous variants, we showed that pathogenicity can be extrapolated between them with 95% accuracy or up to 98% accuracy for closer homologs. These findings expand the repertoire of clinically annotated variants and offer a method for predicting the pathogenicity of unannotated variants, enhancing genotype-phenotype correlations in rare genetic disorders diagnosis.
